<h3>TOP STORIES</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>White House framework sets the tone:</strong> The White House released an AI policy framework that gives developers and corporate users a clearer national direction on safety, governance, innovation, and competitiveness, even though many enforcement details still need to be defined. For financial professionals, this matters because policy clarity can influence adoption timelines, compliance spending, and which firms are best positioned to scale AI responsibly. (Source: The National Law Review)</li>
<li><strong>Microsoft, OpenAI, Amazon, Google, and others are leaning on gas-fired power:</strong> AI data center growth is pushing developers toward large natural gas projects to secure reliable electricity, highlighting a major second-order investment theme in utilities, generation, and grid equipment. The risk is that emissions concerns, regulatory scrutiny, and overly aggressive demand forecasts could create stranded-capital or valuation pressure later. (Source: TechCrunch)</li>
<li><strong>Oracle is reportedly cutting jobs while raising AI spending:</strong> Oracle’s reported layoffs of tens of thousands alongside heavier AI investment show how large enterprises are reallocating labor and capital toward cloud and AI priorities. That is a useful signal for advisors because it suggests AI may support long-term growth while also driving near-term restructuring risk across legacy business lines. (Source: TradingKey)</li>
<li><strong>U.S. data center projects are hitting physical bottlenecks:</strong> Roughly half of planned U.S. data center builds have reportedly been delayed or canceled because of power constraints, transformer shortages, and dependence on parts from China. The takeaway for investors is that AI upside may increasingly accrue to electrical equipment, grid modernization, and industrial supply chains, not just chipmakers. (Source: Tom's Hardware)</li>
</ul>
