2. Compute scarcity is broadening from GPUs to an integrated supply-chain constraint spanning high-bandwidth memory, custom silicon, edge AI chips, liquid cooling, power delivery, grid interconnection, and renewable generation capacity.
3. Agent capability is being productized around callable domain tools and workflow orchestration, especially in scientific discovery, risk intelligence, customer experience, and media operations, creating pricing power for vendors that control proprietary data interfaces and regulated process layers.

Second-Order Effects:
1. AI capex will increasingly be judged by utilization and return on invested infrastructure, making power availability, permitting friction, community resistance, and token-serving efficiency material valuation variables for cloud, data center, semiconductor, and utility-linked equities.
2. Enterprise AI monetization is likely to migrate toward vertical platforms and middleware that own governance, auditability, data integration, and workflow control, pressuring generic chatbot offerings while expanding opportunity for compliance software, scientific computing, cybersecurity, and industry-specific SaaS.
3. National AI strategies will amplify semiconductor and energy supply-chain localization, raising demand for domestic memory, accelerators, grid assets, and power equipment while increasing geopolitical fragmentation and substitution risk for globally concentrated chip and cloud suppliers.
