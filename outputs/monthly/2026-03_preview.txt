Monthly Tunes from the Gen AI Songbook

Month of March 2026

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
- March’s dominant signal was a clear shift from AI as a software narrative to AI as a physical-capacity and infrastructure cycle, with power, cooling, memory, networking, permitting, and site access repeatedly cited as gating factors.
- The investable AI universe broadened meaningfully beyond the largest platform names into utilities, grid equipment, cooling, optical networking, memory, design tools, cloud infrastructure, and industrial automation.
- Enterprise adoption also matured: the emphasis moved from pilots and copilots toward measurable workflow automation, back-office ROI, governance layers, and human-in-the-loop deployment in regulated settings.
- Labor signals turned more tangible but remained mixed in timing: hiring restraint, selective layoffs, and workflow redesign appeared more consistently than evidence of immediate economy-wide job destruction.
- Markets became more selective on monetization and returns, rewarding booked demand and operational proof points while questioning capital intensity, execution risk, and whether AI revenue growth converts into durable margins.

STRUCTURAL WINNERS
1. Compute, power, and physical AI infrastructure
   This was the highest-conviction recurring theme across the month and the top weekly signal by late March. Power availability, cooling, land, permitting, and grid access increasingly appeared as strategic bottlenecks, supporting exposure not only to semis and cloud, but also utilities, electrical equipment, thermal management, and facility operators.

2. Broader semiconductor enablers beyond flagship GPU vendors
   Memory, wafer capacity, optical networking, photonics, interconnect, and chip design tools repeatedly surfaced as monetizing layers of the stack. The signal strengthened as bottlenecks expanded beyond GPUs alone, suggesting a wider “picks and shovels” opportunity set but also more uneven winners across the supply chain.

3. Industrial automation and asset uptime
   Weekly conviction rose sharply here by month-end, supported by recurring examples of AI improving throughput, predictive maintenance, and operational efficiency in factories and asset-heavy environments. This points to AI capex spreading from IT budgets into operations and industrial spending with clearer cost-justification.

4. Software-defined sensing on installed infrastructure
   Software-defined sensing and analytics on existing systems emerged as a durable adoption path because it lowers deployment friction and hardware intensity. The recurring message was that AI can create value by upgrading buildings, networks, and industrial assets already in place, favoring software, services, and data ownership models.

RISING THEMES
1. AI factory economics and inference-led buildout
   The month increasingly framed AI as full-stack infrastructure rather than model training alone. Messaging around inference, tokens per watt, networking, storage, and integrated system design suggests spending may continue to widen across data center supply chains rather than remain concentrated in accelerators.

2. Governance, auditability, and control layers
   Governance moved from side issue to core adoption requirement. Repeated references to approvals, permissions, audit trails, compliance platforms, and sector-specific risk frameworks indicate that a growing share of enterprise AI spend may accrue to oversight, security, and workflow-control software.

3. Measurable enterprise workflow automation
   Adoption signals improved where ROI was concrete: procurement, compliance, coding assistance, financial services workflows, and legal or administrative processes. The recurring pattern was less “AI experimentation” and more task redesign inside core systems, which supports software vendors tied to embedded workflow productivity.

4. Labor impact through hiring slowdown and staffing mix changes
   The labor theme strengthened through repeated evidence of hiring freezes, selective restructuring, and higher productivity expectations. The signal still points more to slower entry-level hiring and role redesign than immediate mass displacement, but it is becoming increasingly relevant to margin narratives and workforce-sensitive sectors.

BREAKING DOWN
1. Pure software-excitement framing
   By month-end, the emphasis had clearly shifted away from AI as primarily an application-layer story. Physical constraints and real-world deployment economics increasingly dominated coverage, reducing the usefulness of valuation frameworks based only on user growth or product visibility.

2. Assumptions that AI demand automatically converts into profits
   Several reports showed strong AI revenue demand paired with margin pressure, heavier capex, or weak stock reactions. The market is becoming less willing to reward AI exposure without evidence of disciplined spending, backlog conversion, and earnings durability.

3. Confidence in frictionless data center expansion
   Multiple stories highlighted canceled or delayed projects, environmental scrutiny, financing strain, and local political resistance. This weakens the assumption that announced AI infrastructure demand will convert smoothly into deployed capacity on expected timelines.

4. Near-term expectations for broad labor replacement
   While labor disruption remained a live theme, the month’s evidence was more consistent with gradual hiring freezes, targeted layoffs, and human-in-the-loop redesign than immediate economy-wide job elimination.

PORTFOLIO IMPLICATIONS
- Keep core exposure aligned with infrastructure bottlenecks rather than only end-user applications: power, cooling, grid equipment, memory, optics, networking, and semiconductor design tools remained the most persistent cross-source beneficiaries.
- Favor companies showing contracted demand, backlog visibility, or measurable operating ROI over those relying mainly on aspirational AI narratives; the month’s signal increasingly rewarded monetization quality and capital discipline.
- Industrial and asset-heavy adopters deserve more attention as AI spending broadens into factory automation, predictive maintenance, and infrastructure optimization with clearer payback logic than some consumer or office-productivity themes.
- Add governance and control-layer beneficiaries to the AI opportunity set, especially in regulated industries where adoption appears to depend on auditability, permissions, and human oversight.
- Treat labor and policy as second-order portfolio variables: hiring restraint, selective layoffs, permitting risk, and sector-specific regulation are becoming more relevant to margins, adoption timing, and regional infrastructure winners.
