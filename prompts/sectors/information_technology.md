## Sector Adapter: Information Technology

- Sector definition: IT services, software, communications equipment, technology hardware, storage, peripherals, electronic equipment, instruments, components, semiconductors, and semiconductor equipment.
- Main business models: Subscription and usage revenue, professional services, maintenance and support, hardware unit sales, semiconductor unit sales, equipment revenue, tool licenses, IP royalties, and recurring platform or service contracts.
- Value chain notes: Product design, software development, implementation, customer support, infrastructure deployment, chip design, fabrication, packaging, testing, hardware distribution, and installed-base monetization all matter.
- Key revenue drivers: Enterprise and hyperscaler tech spend, AI infrastructure demand, seat and usage expansion, device and hardware refresh cycles, end-market unit demand, pricing power, customer retention, and design win momentum.
- Key cost centers: R&D, sales and marketing, cloud hosting, customer support, implementation labor, fabrication capex, equipment depreciation, inventory, packaging, and channel incentives.
- High-probability Gen AI use cases: Software copilots, support automation, code generation and testing assist, implementation acceleration, IT service delivery productivity, design documentation, EDA workflow assistance, demand planning support, sales engineering productivity, and knowledge retrieval across complex product portfolios.
- Adoption bottlenecks: Inference cost, feature commoditization, security and privacy concerns, validation requirements for critical systems, long hardware and chip design cycles, export controls, concentration risk in AI infrastructure demand, and proving durable ROI beyond novelty.
- Likely beneficiaries: Platforms with entrenched distribution and proprietary workflow data, scaled IT service providers improving labor leverage, infrastructure and networking vendors exposed to AI build-outs, semiconductor and equipment suppliers with real participation in accelerated computing, and vendors selling enabling tools into design and deployment workflows.
- Incumbents at risk: Single-feature software with weak moats, labor-intensive service models with limited differentiation, hardware vendors exposed to commoditization without software or ecosystem control, and technology suppliers with little exposure to the highest-value AI control points.
- Important KPIs or signals investors should watch: AI attach rates, net revenue retention, backlog quality, utilization, gross margin after inference cost, cloud and hyperscaler spend trends, design win commentary, support cost per customer, release velocity, and product mix by AI-related demand.
- Sector-specific analytical traps to avoid: Do not reduce the sector to only software or only semiconductors; do not assume every AI feature supports higher pricing; do not ignore compute cost, cyclicality, and customer concentration; do not confuse user engagement or pilot activity with durable monetization and defensibility.
